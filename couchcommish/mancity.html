The English Premier League: Is Monotony Threatening the Future?

Brandon Novarro | May 30, 2023 • 4 min

Another football season has wrapped up, and the panorama appears remarkably similar. Manchester City has claimed the English Premier League title for the third consecutive season. Concurrently, Bayern Munich has secured their 11th straight Bundesliga title, and Paris Saint-Germain has raised the Ligue 1 trophy for a record 11th time.

Manchester City: Pioneers of a New League Order?

Over the past three seasons, Manchester City has left a lasting impact on the Premier League. Their supremacy has been so extensive that it provokes questions about the league's competitiveness. The English Premier League has traditionally been commended for its unpredictability, with any team possessing the potential to triumph on any given day. A glance back at the Premier League over the previous decade reveals a multitude of clubs ascending to the top, including Liverpool, Chelsea, Leicester City, who was 5000-1 to win the title, and both Manchester clubs. However, Manchester City's recent string of titles suggests an unsettling shift towards one-club dominance.

Is the EPL's Competitive Spirit Waning?

Despite Manchester City’s fifth EPL title in the past six seasons, this season's unexpected triumphs and stunning performances give some room to argue for the continued competitiveness of the league. Manchester City's success largely owes to Norwegian striker Erling Haaland's record-breaking single-season score of 36 goals. At the same time, clubs like Brighton & Hove Albion have exceeded expectations.

Significantly, Manchester United returned to the Champions League in Erik ten Hag's debut season, and Newcastle United qualified for the Champions League for the first time in two decades. Liverpool, in contrast, failed to secure Champions League qualification for the first time since the 2015-16 season, despite an impressive 7-0 victory over rivals Manchester United.

Investment-heavy Chelsea, unfortunately, experienced one of their worst Premier League seasons, culminating in their lowest league finish (12th) since 1993-94. Aston Villa, on the other hand, qualified for European football for the first time since the 2009-10 season, proving the league's potential for transformation and progress.

Remarkably, newly promoted Bournemouth, predicted for relegation at the season's start, secured safety and finished in a comfortable 10th place. Conversely, Southampton found themselves relegated after a series of underwhelming performances. However, some things did not change, with Tottenham continuing to pull a Tottenham and disappoint yet again this year.

The most dramatic storyline, however, belonged to Arsenal, who appeared poised to clinch their first EPL title since 03-04. They began with a five-game unbeaten streak, maintained a lead over Manchester City for much of the season, and were even five points clear of them in March. However, a series of draws allowed Manchester City to close the gap, eventually surpassing Arsenal following a decisive 4-1 victory at the Etihad Stadium. Arsenal's subsequent defeat to Nottingham Forest confirmed Manchester City's 3rd consecutive league title and continued EPL dominance.

A Tale of Two Leagues

This shift towards single-club supremacy isn't unique to the Premier League. In Germany, Bayern Munich's hold on the Bundesliga is well-known. This season, however, Borussia Dortmund nearly dethroned the perennial champions, winning 22 matches, drawing 5, and losing 7. They matched Bayern Munich on points, but a lower goal difference of +39, compared to Bayern's +54, saw Dortmund finish second.

In France, Paris Saint-Germain's predictable march to their 11th Ligue 1 title highlights their total control over the French top flight in recent years.

Football's Universal Challenge: Overcoming Monotony

The absence of competition at the top isn't just a problem for individual leagues; it's a dilemma for football as a whole. Consistent victories by the same teams risk rendering the sport predictable and, consequently, less exciting. The true thrill of football lies in its uncertainty. However, when a handful of clubs persistently dominate their leagues, this uncertainty drastically diminishes.

Looking Forward: The Future of the Premier League and Beyond

The future of the Premier League and European football as a whole remains uncertain. If the trend of single-club dominance continues, it could drastically impact these leagues' competitiveness and allure.

To maintain interest and excitement, these leagues must strive for greater parity. This may involve measures such as more stringent financial fair play rules or implementing a salary cap. Absent such interventions, the beautiful game risks becoming a predictable march.

In conclusion, football fans worldwide yearn for a return to the unpredictability that makes this sport so enthralling. As we anticipate the next season, we hope for a renewed sense of competition and unpredictability in the English Premier League and other top European leagues. For the love of the sport, let's hope that's the case.
